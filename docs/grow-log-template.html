What to track in a mushroom grow log (and why)

The point of a grow log isn't to fill in cells — it's to answer three questions later: which batches yielded best, which are running late, and what keeps contaminating? These columns are the minimum that let you answer all three. They're also exactly what the Mycro Grow Log records.

ColumnWhy it earns its place
Batch ID / labelOne row per block or bag so you can trace a result back to a specific grow.
Species & strainTimelines and yields differ hugely by species — you can only benchmark like against like.
SubstrateMasters Mix, straw, supplemented sawdust — the biggest lever on yield and contamination.
Spawn type / ratioHigher spawn ratios colonize faster; logging it explains why two blocks finished days apart.
Inoculation dateDay 0. Every "days in stage" and overdue flag is measured from here.
Dry substrate weightThe denominator for biological efficiency. Weigh it dry, before hydrating.
Colonized dateMarks the end of the spawn run — compare it to the typical range to catch a slow block.
Pinning / fruiting dateWhen you introduced fruiting conditions and pins appeared — tunes your timing next round.
Harvest date & fresh yieldThe payoff. Log each flush; fresh weight (grams or lb) drives the BE calculation.
Biological efficiency (%)Fresh yield ÷ dry substrate × 100 — the one number that tells you if a grow was good.
Contaminated? + cause/stageNote green mold, bacteria, the stage it showed. Tallying causes is how you fix your loss rate.
NotesAnything unusual — a temperature swing, a new supplier, a strain quirk. Future-you will want it.

The one calculation people get wrong: biological efficiency

Biological efficiency (BE) is how you compare a good grow to a bad one on a level field. The formula:

BE = fresh yield ÷ dry substrate weight × 100
Example: 1.2 kg of fresh oysters from a 1.5 kg dry block = 80% BE. Oysters often run 75–100%+, lion's mane ~50–75%, shiitake ~75–125%.

In a spreadsheet you add a formula column (=fresh_yield/dry_weight*100) and keep your units consistent. It's easy to fumble — mixing grams and kilos, or forgetting to weigh the substrate dry — which is the single most common reason a manual log gives numbers you can't trust.
