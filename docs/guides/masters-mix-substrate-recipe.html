Master's Mix substrate recipe

Master's Mix is the workhorse sawdust-block substrate for gourmet mushrooms — 50% hardwood sawdust, 50% soybean hull, and nothing exotic. Here's the exact recipe, the weights for a real batch, why it fruits so well, and the two mistakes that ruin it.

The recipe, in one line

50% hardwood sawdust · 50% soybean hull · ~1% gypsum · hydrate to field capacity · sterilize

Master's Mix is equal parts hardwood sawdust pellets and soybean hull pellets by dry weight — a 1:1 ratio — with a small amount of gypsum. That's the whole formula. The soy hull is what makes it special: it supplies the nitrogen supplementation, so you don't add wheat bran the way you would with plain supplemented sawdust. Both ingredients come as cheap, clean, compressed pellets (hardwood fuel pellets and soybean hull pellets), which is part of why the mix is so popular — no loose sawdust to source, and the pellets are effectively pre-dried and consistent.

Ingredients & ratios

IngredientAmount (by dry weight)Role
Hardwood sawdust pellets50%Bulk lignocellulose — the carbon the mycelium eats
Soybean hull pellets50%Nitrogen supplementation — drives yield and colonization speed
Gypsum (calcium sulfate)~1% (optional)Buffers pH, keeps the mix from clumping, adds calcium
Water~1.2–1.5× the dry weightHydrate to field capacity (~60% moisture)

Some growers run 2:1 sawdust-to-soy-hull to lower the nitrogen (less contamination risk on marginal sterilization) or 1:2 for a richer mix — but 50/50 is the classic, and it's the ratio most gourmet farms default to.

A real batch: exact weights

Here's a 10 lb dry batch that yields roughly 25 lb of hydrated substrate — about five 5 lb fruiting blocks:

Hardwood sawdust pellets5.0 lb
Soybean hull pellets5.0 lb
Gypsum (~1%)~0.1 lb (2–3 tbsp)
Water (to field capacity)~15 lb (≈1.8 gal)
Hydrated total~25 lb → ~5 blocks

Step by step

  1. Weigh the pellets 50/50. Equal dry weights of hardwood sawdust and soybean hull pellets. Add ~1% gypsum if you're using it.
  2. Add water and mix. Roughly 1.2–1.5 parts water per part of dry pellets. Hot or boiling water speeds the pellets breaking down into loose substrate. Stir until it's uniform.
  3. Rest 20–30 minutes. Soy hulls swell slowly and keep drinking after the sawdust has soaked. Give it time before you judge the moisture.
  4. Check field capacity with the squeeze test. Grab a handful and squeeze hard: a few drops should come out, not a stream. Too dry? Add water. Streaming? Add dry pellets. (More on this in the field-capacity guide.)
  5. Fill your bags. Pack filter-patch grow bags, usually ~5 lb of hydrated substrate per block, and fold the tops loosely.
  6. Sterilize — don't pasteurize. 15 psi (~250°F / 121°C) for at least 2.5 hours. Master's Mix is too rich to pasteurize safely (see below).
  7. Cool completely, sealed. Let bags return to room temperature before opening them. Opening a warm bag pulls in contaminated air as it cools.
  8. Inoculate at ~20% spawn. In front of a flow hood or in a still-air box, mix in colonized grain spawn at about 20% of substrate weight (the spawn-ratio guide covers when to go higher), seal, and set it to colonize.

Why it works so well

The magic of Master's Mix is the balance: hardwood gives the fungus the woody carbon it evolved to eat, and soybean hull adds just enough nitrogen to push fast, dense colonization and heavy fruiting — without the fat, contamination-prone supplementation of something like a high-bran mix. On oyster and lion's mane it routinely delivers high biological efficiency, which is why nearly every commercial gourmet farm runs some version of it.

The two mistakes that ruin a batch

  • Pasteurizing instead of sterilizing. This is the #1 Master's Mix failure. The soy hull that makes it yield also makes it a feast for mold and bacteria. A hot-water or lime bath (fine for plain straw) leaves survivors that overrun the rich mix. It must be pressure-sterilized.
  • Too wet. Overshooting field capacity leaves water pooling at the bottom of the bag, which goes anaerobic and sour and stalls colonization. When in doubt, aim slightly drier and let the squeeze test — not a number — be the judge.
