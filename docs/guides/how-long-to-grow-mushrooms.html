How long does it take to grow mushrooms?

Short answer: most gourmet mushrooms go from inoculation to first harvest in 2–5 weeks — but it ranges from about 3 weeks for oyster to 3+ months for shiitake. Here's the full timeline, stage by stage, with real day counts for 14 species.

The four stages of a grow

Every mushroom grow moves through the same four stages. The species mostly changes how long each one takes — not the order.

  1. Inoculation (day 0). You mix colonized grain spawn into a sterilized or pasteurized substrate (sawdust, Masters Mix, straw). The clock starts here.
  2. Colonization / spawn run. White mycelium spreads through the whole block and claims it. This is the longest and most variable stage — from ~10 days for oyster to 5–10 weeks for shiitake.
  3. Pinning (primordia). Once fully colonized and exposed to fresh air, light, and high humidity, the block forms tiny mushroom "pins." Usually 3–7 days after you introduce fruiting conditions.
  4. Fruiting to harvest. Pins mature into full mushrooms. For most gourmet species that's another 4–10 days. Then you harvest, rest the block, and it flushes again.

The big split: oyster and lion's mane are the fast lane (~3–4 weeks, forgiving, great for beginners), while shiitake and maitake are the slow lane (2–3+ months, because the block must fully mature before it fruits). If you want mushrooms soon, start with oyster.

What actually determines the timeline

Two grows of the same species can finish weeks apart. The levers that move your timeline:

  • Temperature. The single biggest factor. Mycelium colonizes fastest at the species' optimum and slows sharply when the room is cold — a few degrees low can add a week to the spawn run.
  • Spawn ratio. More spawn points = less distance for the mycelium to cover = faster colonization. A 30% block can finish days ahead of a 10% one. See the spawn ratio guide.
  • Substrate hydration. Too dry starves the mycelium; too wet suffocates it and invites contamination. Hydrate to field capacity.
  • Species & strain. Some strains are simply faster colonizers. Shiitake's long timeline is biology, not a mistake — it needs to brown before it fruits.
  • Fresh air & light at the right moment. Introducing fruiting conditions too early wastes days; too late and the block can start pinning inside the bag.

It doesn't stop at the first harvest

A colonized block isn't a one-shot. After the first flush you rest it, rehydrate if needed, and it fruits again — usually every 1–2 weeks for oysters — giving 2–4 flushes before it's spent. So the real question isn't just "how long to the first mushroom" but "how many weeks of harvests will this block give me." Tracking flush dates and weights is how you learn each block's real productive life on your farm.

Track your own timeline — it's the only accurate one

The day counts above are typical ranges. Your numbers — how fast your strains colonize in your space, when they pin, how many flushes you get — are the ones that let you plan production and spot problems early (a block running 5 days slow is usually the first sign something's off). The only way to know them is to log the date at each stage.
